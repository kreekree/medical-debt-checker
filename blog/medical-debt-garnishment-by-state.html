Medical Debt Garnishment by State: What Creditors Can Actually Do in 2026

By WageGarnishmentLaws.com · April 13, 2026 · 10 min read

Over $88 billion in medical debt is held by American households. For millions of people, a hospital bill or emergency room visit becomes a years-long collection battle — one that can end in a lawsuit and wage garnishment. Yet most patients don't know the rules: what collectors can do, when they can do it, and what rights they have to fight back.

The short answer to "can medical debt lead to wage garnishment?" is yes — in most states. But the process is more structured than many people realize, and several states have enacted meaningful protections in recent years. This guide explains how medical debt collection works, how it leads to garnishment, and where the strongest state protections exist in 2026.

How Medical Debt Becomes a Wage Garnishment

Medical debt starts as a bill from a hospital, physician, or other provider. Unlike a credit card — where you signed a credit agreement and the terms are explicit — medical debt often arises without any prior agreement about payment. You went to an ER, received care, and later received a bill you couldn't pay. That's the reality for tens of millions of Americans each year.

Here's the typical path from unpaid bill to garnishment:

  1. Internal collections (0–6 months): The provider's billing department attempts to collect. They may offer payment plans or financial assistance. Nonprofit hospitals are legally required to offer charity care to qualifying patients — but you typically have to ask for it.
  2. Sold to a collection agency (6–12 months): Unpaid accounts are often sold to a third-party debt collector, typically for pennies on the dollar. The collector then attempts to collect the full amount. At this point, the original medical provider is usually out of the picture.
  3. Collections contact (ongoing): The collection agency calls, writes letters, and may report the debt to credit bureaus. Under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), collectors cannot harass you, call at unreasonable hours, or make false statements. You can request that they communicate only in writing.
  4. Lawsuit filed: If collection efforts fail and the debt is large enough to justify legal costs, the collector files a lawsuit in civil court. You are served with a summons and have — in most states — 20–30 days to respond. This is the critical intervention point where many debtors miss their window.
  5. Default judgment: If you don't respond, the court enters a default judgment automatically. This is how the vast majority of debt collection judgments are obtained — the debtor doesn't show up, and the creditor wins by default. No hearing is needed once a default is entered.
  6. Garnishment order: With a judgment in hand, the creditor can apply for a garnishment order, which instructs your employer to withhold a portion of each paycheck and send it directly to the creditor.

The key intervention points are steps 4 and 5 — responding to the lawsuit and negotiating before judgment. Once a judgment is entered, stopping garnishment becomes significantly harder and more expensive.

States Where Medical Debt Wage Garnishment Is Prohibited or Limited

A growing number of states have enacted laws specifically addressing medical debt collection, though most focus on credit reporting rather than garnishment. Here's the current picture for wage garnishment specifically:

States with the strongest garnishment protections (all consumer debt, including medical):

  • Texas: Consumer wage garnishment is prohibited entirely for private debts. A judgment creditor cannot garnish wages in Texas, period. They can pursue bank account levies and property liens, but wages are off-limits.
  • Pennsylvania: Wages are exempt from garnishment for most private consumer debts, including medical bills.
  • North Carolina: Similar broad wage exemption for consumer debts.
  • South Carolina: Similar broad wage exemption for consumer debts.
  • Illinois: Caps garnishment at 15% of gross wages — significantly lower than the federal 25% limit.
  • Delaware: Caps garnishment at 15% of disposable income.
  • Hawaii: Uses a tiered formula that effectively limits garnishment to much less than 25% for most earners.

Recent Changes to Medical Debt Law in 2025–2026

The regulatory landscape around medical debt has shifted significantly in the last two years. Here's what's changed:

Credit reporting: In early 2025, the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) stopped reporting medical debts under $500 on credit reports. The CFPB under the Biden administration proposed a rule to remove all medical debt from credit reports, though the rule's status depends on regulatory developments in 2026.

CFPB oversight: The CFPB has increased scrutiny of medical debt collectors, including actions against collectors who misrepresented debt amounts, continued collection on debts that should have been covered by financial assistance programs, or failed to honor dispute rights.

State-level protections: Several states have passed laws specifically targeting aggressive medical debt collection. Colorado enacted legislation capping interest on medical debt at 8% and requiring hospitals to proactively offer financial assistance. New York expanded its Medical Debt Protection Act. Nevada restricted certain garnishment actions on medical debt.

Nonprofit hospital requirements: Nonprofit hospitals — which receive significant tax benefits — are required under the Affordable Care Act (ACA) to have financial assistance policies (charity care). Hospitals that fail to offer charity care to qualifying patients and then pursue aggressive collection may violate both the ACA and state law. If you received care at a nonprofit hospital and weren't informed about financial assistance, this is worth investigating.

Your Rights as a Medical Debtor

Beyond garnishment law, several federal and state rights apply specifically to medical debt collection:

Right to itemized billing: You can request an itemized bill showing every charge. Medical billing errors are common — studies suggest 30–80% of medical bills contain errors. An itemized bill is your starting point for disputing inaccurate charges.

Right to dispute the debt: Under the FDCPA, within 30 days of first contact from a collector, you can request written validation of the debt. The collector must stop collection activities until they provide it. This doesn't make the debt go away, but it pauses collection and ensures the amount is verified.

Right to a cease-and-desist: You can send a written request asking a collector to stop contacting you. They must comply (with limited exceptions). This doesn't stop the debt but halts collection calls and letters.

Right to financial assistance: If your care was at a nonprofit hospital, ask about charity care or financial assistance programs. Income-based assistance programs at many hospitals can reduce or eliminate bills for qualifying patients — but you typically have to apply, and there are deadlines.

Right to respond to a lawsuit: This is the most critical right and the most commonly waived by default. If you receive a summons, respond. Even a brief, pro se answer stating that you dispute the debt forces the creditor to prove their case and gives you leverage to negotiate. Free legal aid is available in most states for consumers facing debt collection lawsuits — see LawHelp.org.

What to Do If You're Facing Medical Debt Collection

If you're dealing with medical debt collection, here's the recommended sequence:

  1. Request an itemized bill and verify the charges. Dispute any errors in writing within 30 days of first contact from a collector.
  2. Check if you qualify for financial assistance. If the care was at a nonprofit hospital, call the billing department and ask about charity care programs. Apply for any programs you may qualify for.
  3. Check the statute of limitations. Use our state checker to see how long collectors have to sue in your state. If the debt is old, it may be time-barred.
  4. Respond to any lawsuit. If you receive a summons, do not ignore it. File a written answer by the deadline — even a simple one stating you dispute the debt. Seek free legal aid if needed.
  5. Negotiate a settlement. If the debt is valid and collectible, negotiate before a judgment is entered. Post-judgment, your leverage decreases significantly.
  6. Know your state's exemptions. If a garnishment order is issued, check whether your state has exemptions that reduce or eliminate the garnishable amount, and file a claim of exemption if applicable.
