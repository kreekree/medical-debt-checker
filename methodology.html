Primary Data Sources

Federal Law

Consumer Credit Protection Act (CCPA) — Title III

The federal garnishment cap (lesser of 25% of disposable earnings or 30× the federal minimum wage) is established under 15 U.S.C. § 1673. This is the baseline that applies in all states — state laws can only be more protective, not less. Administered and enforced by the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division.

State Statutes

State Garnishment & Execution Laws

Each state's garnishment limit, exemptions, and procedures are sourced from the state's own civil procedure statutes. Key examples: California Code of Civil Procedure §§ 706.010–706.154; Illinois Code of Civil Procedure 735 ILCS 5/12-803; Texas Property Code § 42.001; New York Civil Practice Law & Rules Article 52. State statutes are accessed via official state legislature websites.

Statute of Limitations

State Limitations Periods for Written Contracts

Limitation periods for consumer debt are sourced from each state's statute of limitations law for written contracts or open accounts. These vary by state and by debt type. The periods shown reflect the most common limitation applicable to credit cards and medical debt in each state as of the last review date. Cross-referenced against the CFPB's debt collection resources and National Consumer Law Center publications.

Legal Aid Resources

State Court Self-Help Centers & Legal Aid Networks

The official resource links shown for each state are sourced from official state court websites and legal aid organizations listed in the Legal Services Corporation's grantee directory and LawHelp.org. Only official .gov and established nonprofit legal aid sites are linked.

What Each Data Point Means

Field Definition
Max garnishment % The maximum percentage of disposable earnings that can be withheld per pay period under state law (or the federal default if no state law applies). Lower percentages reflect more protective state laws.
Statute of limitations The number of years a creditor generally has to file a civil lawsuit to collect a consumer debt. After this period, the debt is time-barred. Applies to the most common consumer debt types (credit cards, medical bills). Other debt types may have different periods.
Lawsuit required Whether a creditor must obtain a court judgment before garnishing wages. "Yes" for all private consumer debts in most states. Federal debts (IRS, student loans) are exceptions that can garnish without a judgment in any state.
Protection level A summary classification: Prohibited (no consumer wage garnishment), Strong (cap at ≤15% or significant exemptions), Moderate (state-specific rules below federal cap), Federal (follows the federal 25% default).

Review & Update Process

State garnishment laws change through legislative action. We review all state data annually and update when we become aware of statutory changes. Notable recent changes are flagged in state-specific notes within the tool.

If you believe a data point is outdated or incorrect, please contact us with the relevant state statute citation. We investigate all reported corrections within 48 hours.

Known Limitations

  • ⚠Not legal advice. This tool provides general legal information, not advice tailored to your individual situation. Laws are applied differently by courts and individual facts matter enormously in debt collection cases.
  • ⚠Federal debt exceptions. IRS tax levies, federal student loan garnishments, and child support/alimony orders follow federal or separate state rules and are not covered by the standard state garnishment limits shown in this tool.
  • ⚠Local variations. Some counties and cities have additional procedures or exemptions. Court discretion also plays a role in how garnishment orders are issued and enforced locally.
  • ⚠Laws change. Statutes are amended by legislatures. While we review data annually, a law may have changed since our last update. Always verify with a licensed attorney before relying on this information for a legal decision.
