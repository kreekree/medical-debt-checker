About This Site

Our Mission

WageGarnishmentLaws.com is a free, independent resource built to help Americans understand their rights before creditors come calling.

"Most Americans don't know their state's garnishment rules until a creditor has already obtained a judgment. By then, their options are limited. We built this tool so people can understand the process — and their protections — before it's urgent."

What We Do

WageGarnishmentLaws.com provides a free, instant lookup of wage garnishment rules for all 50 states and Washington D.C. Enter your state, debt type, and employment status — and within seconds you'll see your state's maximum garnishment percentage, the statute of limitations on consumer debt claims, whether a lawsuit is required first, and links to official state legal resources.

We cover medical debt, credit card debt, personal loans, and other common consumer debts. We do not cover student loans, federal tax debt, or child support orders — these follow separate federal rules not determined by state law.

Why We Built This

Medical debt is the leading cause of personal bankruptcy in the United States. Over $88 billion in medical debt is held by American households, and millions of people face collection actions each year without knowing what creditors can legally do — or what protections their state provides.

Many people don't respond to debt collection lawsuits simply because they don't understand the process or don't realize they have rights worth asserting. A default judgment — entered when someone doesn't respond — gives creditors the legal authority to garnish wages, levy bank accounts, and place liens on property. In states like Texas, Pennsylvania, North Carolina, and South Carolina, consumer wage garnishment is prohibited entirely. In Illinois and Delaware, caps are significantly lower than the federal limit. These are facts people deserve to know before a judgment is entered.

We built this tool to provide clear, accurate, state-specific information at the moment people need it most.

Accuracy & Limitations

State laws change. We review data periodically and update when legislatures amend garnishment statutes. However, this site is not a substitute for legal advice — laws may have changed since our last update, and individual circumstances vary significantly. Always verify current law with a licensed attorney in your state before making legal decisions.
